01 · Context

A compliance reporting product facing a state mandate

When I joined PowerSchool, Texas state reporting in eSchoolPlus ran on XML-based batch submissions. Districts generated reporting extracts, uploaded them to state systems, and waited for processing results before they could spot issues. It had worked for years. But the regulatory landscape was shifting, and what had been a workable model was about to become a compliance liability.

Through its TSDS Upgrade Project — announced September 2021 — TEA mandated a shift away from XML toward Ed-Fi, a nationally adopted interoperability standard replacing file-based submissions with real-time API transactions flowing directly into TEA's Hosted Ed-Fi ODS. By August 2024, XML would no longer be accepted. Period.

Texas wasn't a small edge case. Over 40 active eSchoolPlus districts relied on the product to meet legally mandated PEIMS submissions ten times per school year— a cadence that has since expanded further with TEA's addition of new submission types beginning in the 2025–26 school year.. Missing a deadline directly impacts district accountability ratings and state funding calculations. Three risks came into focus immediately:

Compliance risk
Districts failing to transition before TEA's August 2024 go-live would miss mandatory submissions determining funding and accountability ratings.
Business risk
Texas represented a critical revenue stream for eSchoolPlus. At least one competing district had already switched to PowerSchool, citing gaps in a competitor's Texas reporting capability.
Technical debt risk
Maintaining a legacy XML framework while also supporting Ed-Fi would compound engineering complexity and slow every future reporting enhancement.

The real question wasn't whether we'd move to Ed-Fi — TEA had settled that. The question was whether we'd modernise strategically or keep investing in technology the state had already decided to retire.

02 · Discovery

Asking first — before touching the roadmap

Before making any roadmap recommendation, I worked with customers, our US counterpart product manager, support teams, engineering, and state reporting experts to understand what the transition actually meant in practice. The technical mandate was clear. The human impact required more careful listening.

Customer conversations surfaced three consistent concerns: confidence that PowerSchool would be ready before TEA's deadlines, visibility into reporting status and validation errors as data moved through the system, and minimal disruption during the migration itself. None of those are really about Ed-Fi — they're about trust and continuity.

Internally, engineering teams evaluated multiple approaches. One proposal suggested maintaining the existing XML engine while building translation layers between XML submissions and Ed-Fi API calls — attractive short-term, but it introduced duplicate processing logic, higher maintenance costs, and ongoing technical debt. You'd be paying twice to produce the same output.

At the same time, PowerSchool had already invested in Data Exchange (DEX), a platform purpose-built for Ed-Fi interoperability and state reporting modernisation. The opportunity was clear: instead of extending legacy architecture, this mandate could establish a scalable foundation that would serve Texas districts for years beyond the compliance deadline. Treating a regulatory constraint as a platform opportunity shaped every decision that followed.

03 · Strategic Choice

Three paths — one clear answer

I presented leadership with three options. The analysis below drove the final decision:

Option A
Maintain existing XML

Keep supporting legacy workflows with incremental XML updates. Lowest short-term investment, but growing compliance risk and zero long-term value. Districts get the same friction with no improvement.

Option B
Hybrid translation layer

Support Ed-Fi by translating API transactions back into XML processes. Faster initial build, but creates dual architectures with higher operating costs and compounding maintenance burden.

Option C
Full DEX & Ed-Fi migration ✓ Selected

Modernise reporting around DEX and direct Ed-Fi publishing. Larger upfront investment — but aligned with TEA's future direction, dramatically reduced technical debt, real-time error checking, and a future-ready architecture with strongly positive customer impact.

Option C was the only path that made sense long-term. The upfront investment was larger — but one-time. The alternative was paying smaller ongoing costs indefinitely while accumulating architecture that would actively constrain future development.

04 · Execution

Building in flight: migrating live districts

The most challenging aspect wasn't building the solution — it was migrating active districts who were submitting legally required data to the state while school was in session onto a completely new reporting framework. Every eSchoolPlus district in Texas had PEIMS deadlines throughout the migration. A failed cutover was not recoverable.

Data element mapping

The foundational work was mapping every PEIMS data element from eSchoolPlus's internal data model to its corresponding Ed-Fi entity, endpoint, and descriptor in TEA's Texas Education Data Standards (TEDS). TEDS is built on the national Ed-Fi core model but includes a Texas Core Extension with state-specific data elements and descriptor namespaces that don't exist in the standard Ed-Fi specification.

This distinction matters. The Ed-Fi standard uses ed-fi.org namespaces. TEA uses a Texas-specific namespace for extended descriptors. Using the wrong namespace produces a descriptor mismatch that fails validation — data doesn't land in the ODS. This was among the most common migration errors across all SIS vendors during the parallel submission year.

The ordering of API calls also required careful sequencing. StudentSchoolAssociation can only be created after the referenced Student and School records already exist. Attendance events require a valid StudentSchoolAssociation. Wrong sequence generates Level 1 API rejections that look identical to data quality errors — which created genuine confusion during early district migrations.

Validation levels and resolution

Level Classification Common triggers Resolution approach
Level 1 API rejection — data doesn't reach ODS Missing required field, invalid descriptor namespace, referential key mismatch (e.g. StudentUniqueId not found) Blocks submission entirely. Must be resolved before any data reaches the ODS landing zone.
Level 1.5 Descriptor filter — data lands but fails TEA filter layer Local code not mapped to TEA Ed-Fi descriptor; using national ed-fi.org namespace instead of Texas Core Extension Ensure descriptor namespaces match TEA's published TEDS descriptor tables exactly.
Level 2 — Fatal PEIMS fatal — data in ODS but fails downstream business rule validation Duplicate StaffService + begin date; speech therapy student missing primary InstructionalSetting; HomelessStatus conflicts Doesn't block the API but blocks PEIMS promotion. Districts see errors in TEA's PEIMS EDIT+ tool after promotion.
Level 2 — Warning Non-fatal — data promoted but flagged for review Attendance percentage anomalies, unusual leaver reasons, demographic inconsistencies Must be reviewed before certification. Don't block submission but can delay sign-off.

The four-phase migration model

1
Parallel Validation
Both systems running simultaneously
DEX-based Ed-Fi publishing was enabled while existing XML workflows remained active. Both systems ran in parallel, producing outputs we compared to validate data mappings, identify edge cases, and resolve discrepancies before any district cutover. This phase caught errors that only surface with real production data.
2
Pilot Rollout
Small cohort, refined playbooks
A small cohort of districts adopted the new model first. Their feedback refined migration playbooks, support processes, and the in-product error visibility tools that districts use to monitor submission status. Publishing workflow, data quality monitoring, operational readiness, and customer adoption were all validated at small scale before broad rollout.
3
Scaled Migration
Full customer base, structured readiness gates
With confidence established from the pilot cohort, we expanded across the full customer base. This required close coordination between engineering, product, customer success, support, and district stakeholders. Each migration followed a structured readiness checklist and validation gate before cutover.
4
Full Adoption
XML retired before August 2024
The final phase completed all remaining migrations, retired legacy XML dependencies, and confirmed every district was successfully operating through DEX before TEA's August 2024 go-live — the point at which XML submissions were permanently retired.

The competitive differentiation wasn't about who built an Ed-Fi connector fastest. It was about who had the depth of Texas-specific knowledge — PEIMS data element history, TEDS descriptor nuances, Texas Core Extension — to migrate districts without disruption. That depth was embedded in eSchoolPlus's product and team. Competitors without that history faced steeper learning curves on the same validation rules we'd spent years understanding.

The August 2024 go-live was not the end of the work — it was the point at which the migration stopped being a project and became the operating standard. In the first full Ed-Fi-only PEIMS cycle (Fall 2024–25), all migrated districts completed their submissions through DEX without reverting to manual workarounds or requiring emergency TEA extensions. The real-time error visibility that DEX provided meant districts identified and resolved data quality issues earlier in the submission window than was possible under the old XML batch model — exactly the operational improvement the migration had been designed to deliver. The infrastructure put in place for Texas also positioned the platform to absorb subsequent TEA submission timeline changes without requiring architecture-level rework.

07 · Takeaways

Four lessons that carry forward

1

Regulatory change is a product opportunity

What initially looked like a compliance burden became the forcing function to modernise a critical product and eliminate years of accumulated technical debt. The constraint created the clarity to do something that would have been hard to justify otherwise.

2

Technical debt compounds faster than it looks

Maintaining a legacy XML engine while supporting Ed-Fi would have cost more — in engineering time, testing coverage, and customer friction — than the upfront investment in a full migration. The short-term savings of a hybrid approach were an illusion.

3

Customer trust is the real success metric

Districts weren't asking for Ed-Fi. They were asking for confidence that they could keep meeting their reporting obligations without disruption. Every product and migration decision was tested against that question.

4

Migrations succeed through risk management, not speed

Running legacy and modernised systems in parallel was slower than a hard cutover. It was also the only approach that gave us the confidence to migrate 40+ active districts without a single missed deadline. Slower was safer — and safer was the right call.

A note on subsequent developments:

The migration work described in this case study concluded in August 2024. In December 2024, PowerSchool experienced a data security incident unrelated to the Ed-Fi migration architecture — affecting customer data across the broader PowerSchool platform. That incident has since influenced district procurement decisions across the industry and is part of the evolving context any K-12 technology decision-maker should be aware of. The technical and operational decisions documented here predate those developments and address a separate domain: state reporting compliance infrastructure, not data security posture.

"The best product decisions aren't about adopting new technology — they're about reducing customer risk while building for what's next."
