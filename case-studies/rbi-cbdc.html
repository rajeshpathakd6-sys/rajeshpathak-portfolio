01 · Context

The question every AI product team eventually faces

The question every product manager building an AI product eventually faces is: how do you know if it's actually working? Not whether the model is mathematically accurate — but whether the thing you're measuring actually matters, and whether the environment you're deploying into can act on what the model tells it.

I ran into both at the same time in 2021, while working at the Reserve Bank of India's Department of Statistics and Information Management in Kolkata. The brief: assess whether India was ready for a Digital Rupee — and generate the data-driven modelling to support that assessment.

Background · What is a CBDC?

Central Bank Digital Currencies are the digital equivalent of a country's physical cash — issued and backed by the central bank, just like paper notes, but in electronic form. Think of it like a government-issued digital wallet instead of physical rupee notes. In 2021, central banks globally were actively exploring CBDCs as the next evolution of sovereign money. While China was already piloting its digital yuan, the RBI was evaluating whether India had the financial infrastructure, regulatory maturity, and digital readiness required for a future Digital Rupee.

My project had three distinct parts — each a compressed version of the same fundamental product challenge:

Task 01 · Segmentation
State readiness model
Build a model scoring which Indian states are ready for a Digital Rupee pilot — defining a meaningful metric where no ground truth existed.
Task 02 · ML Analysis
Global adoption drivers
Identify the global drivers of CBDC adoption across 125 countries using machine learning — surfacing what actually predicts success.
Task 03 · Macro Impact
Money velocity modelling
Quantify how digital currency adoption affects money velocity over 40 years of RBI data — and whether short-run and long-run effects diverge.

It was, in hindsight, a compressed version of every hard product decision I've made since: define the right metric, pick the right model inputs, understand what the output enables — and what it can't — and accept that "the model is accurate" is never the finish line.

02 · Before the Models

Before a single model ran, we had to define what "ready" meant

This is the part of AI product work that gets skipped in demos and rushed in sprints. Before any model runs, someone has to decide what the model is actually trying to predict — and that decision is largely irreversible. Get it wrong and you end up with a very precise answer to the wrong question.

For CBDC readiness, we had no ground truth label. No Indian state had ever launched a digital rupee. So the first task was genuine product management work: define "readiness" as a concept, translate it into measurable variables, and choose stand-in metrics — called proxies — for things we couldn't directly observe.

What is a proxy metric?

A proxy is a substitute measure. When you can't directly measure what you care about, you find something highly correlated with it. Example: a product manager can't directly measure customer satisfaction after every feature interaction. Instead, they track user retention, daily active users, or the return rate as proxy indicators of satisfaction.

The most important global predictor of CBDC adoption — the Financial Development Index — doesn't exist at the Indian state level. So we substituted it with the Financial Inclusion Index: banking penetration, credit availability, and last-mile branch density. Every substitution below was a judgment call the model itself couldn't make:

Ideal variable Proxy used Why
Financial Development Index Financial Inclusion Index FDI not available at state level. FII captures banking penetration, credit access, and rural branch density as close substitutes.
Regulatory quality score Public Affairs Index Measures how effectively state governments administer services and enforce rules — a reasonable stand-in for institutional capacity.
Trade-to-GDP ratio Exports as % of GSDP Standard trade ratio unavailable at state level. Export share of Gross State Domestic Product captures economic openness.
Mobile subscriber count Tele-density (connections per 100 people) Normalises for population size — reflects actual digital infrastructure coverage rather than raw market scale.

Every substitution was a product decision. The model couldn't make them. Only someone who understood both the domain and the dataset could. This is where AI product risk actually lives: not in the algorithm, but in the requirements upstream of it.

03 · Global Analysis

Feature importance is a prioritisation framework in disguise

We ran a Random Forest classifier — a model that builds hundreds of decision trees and averages their outputs for more reliable predictions — across data from 125 countries to identify the top predictors of CBDC adoption. The model achieved 76% precision and 71% recall on a difficult classification problem where labelled examples were sparse.

Precision vs Recall

Precision means: of all the countries the model predicted as "CBDC-ready", how many actually were? 76% means it was right about three-quarters of the time. Recall means: of all the countries that were actually CBDC-ready, how many did the model correctly identify? 71% means it caught most of them.

76%
Precision
Of all countries predicted CBDC-ready, three-quarters were. High-confidence positive predictions on a sparse labelling problem.
71%
Recall
Of all genuinely ready countries, the model identified most — catching real opportunities without excessive false positives.

To contextualise those numbers: a naive majority-class classifier on this dataset — one that simply predicted every country as "not CBDC-ready" based on the preponderance of non-adopters — would have achieved roughly 60–65% accuracy on the raw class distribution. The Random Forest's 76% precision represents meaningful signal above that baseline, not a marginal improvement over chance. But the number I found most valuable wasn't accuracy. It was the feature importance output — a ranking of which variables the model leaned on most heavily to make its predictions. Financial system depth outweighed internet penetration, regulatory quality, and raw GDP.

Countries advancing fastest on CBDC weren't the most technologically sophisticated — they were the ones where financial institutions already had depth, access, and efficiency. Technology is the last layer, not the first. If the institutional foundation isn't in place, a CBDC launch doesn't create it. It exposes its absence.

PM Insight

What your model weights most heavily is a direct signal about where product investment has the highest expected return. The Random Forest was telling policymakers — and me — that if you want to accelerate CBDC adoption, the highest-leverage intervention is financial system development, then digital access infrastructure, then regulatory quality. That is a product roadmap. The model just expressed it in variable importance terms instead of the frameworks product managers typically use.

04 · State Segmentation

The Preparedness Index was a go-to-market segmentation model

The second phase used a multi-step logistic regression framework — building a readiness score iteratively, with each stage incorporating additional predictor variables and re-estimating coefficients against the evolving model — trained on a decade of state-level data (2011–2020) to score CBDC readiness across 14 Indian states.

The output: a preparedness index from 0 to 1. The analysis covered 14 states for which a consistent decade of data across all four proxy dimensions was available at the state level — a data availability constraint rather than a deliberate exclusion of the remaining states and union territories.

Translated into product language, this is a customer segmentation model and a pilot launch sequencing recommendation. The results were striking:

State Score Tier What drove the rating
Kerala 1.0 Launch-ready Ranked above the median on financial inclusion, internet penetration, governance quality, and ATM density — all at the same time, consistently across the full decade.
Maharashtra 1.0 Launch-ready Strong across all four dimensions — financial inclusion depth anchored by Mumbai's banking infrastructure, high tele-density, above-median governance quality, and consistent export intensity as a share of GSDP across the full decade.
Haryana 0.9 High readiness High tele-density and above-median financial inclusion, but governance quality scores showed more variability across the decade than the top two states. A strong candidate for early-wave expansion once pilot learnings are established.
Tamil Nadu 0.9 High readiness Robust banking penetration and a mature export economy, with consistent performance on the Financial Inclusion proxy. Minor gaps in rural last-mile branch density relative to Kerala and Maharashtra held the score fractionally below the top tier.
Gujarat 0.8 High readiness Strong on export intensity and tele-density — the two infrastructure dimensions — but financial inclusion scores, particularly rural credit availability, lagged the top-tier states. High readiness overall, with an identifiable single lever for improvement.
Uttar Pradesh 0.0 Not ready Scored zero — not for a single year, but for the entire decade studied. Lagged on multiple dimensions simultaneously. Not one missing ingredient; systemic under-preparedness.
Bihar 0.0 Not ready
Assam 0.0 Not ready

The gap wasn't random. High-readiness states consistently ranked above the median across all four dimensions simultaneously. The zero-readiness states lagged on all of them at once — which matters, because fixing a single dimension in a systemically under-prepared market doesn't move it into the next tier.

PM Insight · Pilot Sequencing

Pilot in your highest-readiness segment first. Not because you're deprioritising low-readiness markets — but because early adoption in ready markets generates the feedback loops and institutional learning that make later expansion actually work. Pushing a zero-readiness market at launch isn't an opportunity cost. It's a product support and policy credibility risk.

Today's RBI Digital Rupee pilots are being rolled out in selected institutions, banks, states, and use cases rather than attempting nationwide deployment on day one. That mirrors exactly what the readiness framework was built to recommend — introduce new financial infrastructure where institutional capability, digital maturity, and operational readiness already exist, learn rapidly, then expand progressively.

05 · Macro Dynamics

Short-run vs long-run: the metric horizon problem every PM eventually hits

The third component modelled 40 years of RBI macroeconomic data to study how digital currency adoption affects money velocity — the speed at which money changes hands in the economy. We used two advanced statistical techniques: Engle-Granger cointegration and an Error Correction Model.

What is money velocity?

Imagine ₹100 changes hands five times in a year — you pay a shopkeeper, who pays a supplier, who pays workers, and so on. That ₹100 effectively generated ₹500 of economic activity. That's high velocity. If digital currency makes transactions cheaper and faster, it could change how quickly money moves through the economy. Higher velocity generally signals more economic activity; lower velocity can mean people are holding money longer or transacting more efficiently.

The models fit extremely well — explaining 94.5% of variation in narrow money velocity and 99.5% of broad money velocity. But the more practically useful finding was the divergence between short-run and long-run effects:

94.5%
R² — Narrow money velocity
Error Correction Model explains nearly all variation in narrow money (M1) velocity over the full 40-year period.
99.5%
R² — Broad money velocity
Near-perfect fit on broad money (M3) velocity — confirming the structural drivers are captured cleanly.
Short-run
Digital currency dampens velocity — counterintuitively

In the first few years of digital currency adoption, electronic currency had a strong dampening effect on velocity. Digital currency holders transact more efficiently, so money circulates more slowly at first. You might expect digital money to speed things up — but early adopters tend to hold it longer before spending.

Long-run
Digital payments-to-GDP becomes the dominant driver Key finding

Over many years, the digital payments-to-GDP ratio became highly statistically significant, while the short-run coefficient became negligible. Interest rates showed the opposite pattern — minimal short-term, meaningful long-term. Compounding adoption changes what drives the system entirely.

PM Lesson · The Metric Horizon Problem

A feature can look flat in a 30-day cohort window and be building compounding value by month six. A metric that looks healthy at launch can be masking structural decay that only surfaces over the long run. Most product teams optimise for short-term results because sprint cycles and quarterly goals reward it. The error correction model asks: when the system deviates from its equilibrium, what's the speed and direction of correction? That's a question worth asking about every product metric you track — not just macroeconomic time series.

06 · Framework

Four questions for AI products in regulated environments

Working in a regulated environment — where a model's output feeds real policy decisions — makes certain questions unavoidable that consumer product teams can defer indefinitely. Here are the four I carry with me:

1

What are you actually trying to predict — and is your label valid?

When no state had launched a digital rupee, there was no "right answer" to train on. We had to construct the label ourselves. Many AI projects fail here, not in the model. If your label is wrong, no amount of algorithmic sophistication fixes it.

2

Are your proxies measuring what you think they're measuring?

Every substitution we made — tele-density for mobile subscriptions, the Public Affairs Index for regulatory quality — was a decision that could have gone differently. Proxy validation isn't a statistical step. It's a domain knowledge step.

3

What does the model output actually enable — and what does it prevent?

A readiness score of 0.8 for Gujarat doesn't tell you when to launch there, how to sequence rollout within the state, or what infrastructure gaps need addressing first. The model gives you a signal. The PM's job is to translate that signal into a decision — and understand its limits.

4

Are you measuring at the right time horizon?

Short-run and long-run effects can point in opposite directions. A product that initially slows a metric might be building the compounding behaviour that improves it permanently. If you only measure at 30 days, you may kill features that would have succeeded at 180.

Regulated environments make all four questions unavoidable — which is exactly why experience in FinTech, EdTech compliance, or healthcare produces a different kind of product rigour than purely consumer contexts. Not harder or easier: more structurally demanding. When the cost of a wrong prediction is a policy misallocation or a compliance breach, you can't skip proxy validation. The consequences just show up too visibly.

07 · What Happened Next

CBDC: from research to national infrastructure

When I worked on this project in 2021, India's Digital Rupee existed only as a research initiative. Our work focused on one question: how do you decide where, how, and under what conditions a CBDC should be introduced? Over the following four years, the Reserve Bank of India moved from research into implementation — validating nearly every assumption the framework was built on.

1
Research · 2021
Readiness modelling, adoption drivers, rollout prioritisation
My project — building state-level readiness scores, identifying global CBDC adoption drivers using ML, and modelling macroeconomic velocity impacts — contributed to the RBI's broader assessment of whether and how India should pursue a Digital Rupee.
2
Pilot · 2022
RBI publishes the CBDC Concept Note; launches wholesale and retail pilots
The RBI moved from research to experimentation through controlled deployment — in selected institutions and use cases, not nationwide. The rollout logic was consistent with what the preparedness index had been designed to recommend: prioritise markets where institutional capability, digital maturity, and operational readiness already exist, rather than attempting system-wide deployment before those foundations are confirmed. Whether the 2021 research contributed directly to that decision or simply reflected the same sound reasoning that the RBI's own economists arrived at independently, the alignment between the framework and the approach taken is precise enough to be instructive.
3
Expansion · 2023–24
UPI interoperability, offline capability, and programmability
The retail pilot expanded significantly in its registered user base — but the path was not linear, and the non-linearity was itself instructive. In December 2023, the pilot briefly crossed one million daily transactions. By June 2024, that figure had fallen back to approximately 100,000 — largely because the December spike had been driven by participating banks incentivising their own employees to transact through CBDC wallets, rather than by organic consumer adoption. This is precisely the short-run/long-run divergence the macroeconomic model had flagged. Early adopters transacted efficiently and then slowed, not because the product had failed, but because the behavioural equilibrium of a new payment instrument takes longer to establish than a single-month metric suggests. By March 2025, the retail pilot had reached over six million registered users — a number that reflects slower, stickier adoption rather than an incentive-driven spike. The long-run variable, digital payments penetration, was beginning to assert itself. Focus shifted from technical feasibility to user adoption and product maturity — exactly the transition the short-run/long-run model had been built to anticipate.
4
Scale · 2025–26
Programmable CBDC for welfare distribution; cross-border pilots initiated
The Digital Rupee began serving real-world policy functions — targeted welfare distribution and cross-border payment pilots with international partners. Market readiness, rollout sequencing, institutional maturity, and long-term adoption — exactly the questions the 2021 research was designed to answer — had become central to implementation strategy.

"The most consequential decision in any AI project is rarely technical — it's the moment someone chooses what to measure and accepts that the choice is largely irreversible. Get the label wrong, and no algorithm recovers it. Get the proxy wrong, and precision is just a precise answer to the wrong question. At the RBI, that lesson arrived before a single model ran. It hasn't left since."
