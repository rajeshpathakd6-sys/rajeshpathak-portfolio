01 · Executive Summary

Simplifying 160+ products into one subscription

When I joined VMware as an Associate Product Owner, the company was running one of its largest subscription commerce transformations — simplifying a portfolio of more than 160 infrastructure products into a single, streamlined subscription experience.

One of the flagship initiatives behind that transformation was the Unified Cloud Pack program. Instead of buying vSphere, vSAN, and NSX as separate products, customers could now purchase a single subscription bundle with unified licensing, pricing, and renewals — a simple front door to a genuinely complex backend.

My focus: I worked within the Offers Team, owning the product backlog for the Offers domain — primarily Oracle Product Data Hub (PDH) — VMware's enterprise product master data system, which served as the single authoritative source for every commercial offer definition. PDH is where bundles are formally structured: which products belong together, what entitlement rules apply, how pricing tiers are configured, and what subscription terms govern renewals. Before any customer could see a quote or complete a purchase, that offer had to be correctly defined in PDH and validated for downstream consumption by CPQ (configure-price-quote), SAP (contracts and billing), the Commerce portal, and Licensing and Provisioning. Every commercial decision VMware made flowed through this system before it reached a customer.

I didn't own the entire transformation. I owned one critical link in the chain: making sure business requirements were translated into structured product definitions that every downstream system could rely on.

02 · Business Context

A catalog that had outgrown its own complexity

VMware's infrastructure catalog had grown organically for years. Customers buying vSphere for virtualization, vSAN for software-defined storage, NSX for networking, and other products typically bought each one separately — and each came with its own licensing model, pricing structure, entitlement rules, and renewal process. Manageable individually, increasingly hard to manage in combination.

Unified Cloud Pack was VMware's answer: let customers buy multiple complementary products as a single subscription bundle instead of managing each one on its own.

The catch: while customers saw one simple subscription, VMware had to make sure every commercial bundle was represented consistently across product catalogs, quoting systems, finance platforms, commerce applications, and licensing systems. That meant a real transformation of the Subscription Commerce Platform — not just a new SKU.

The urgency of that transformation intensified significantly over the course of my tenure. In May 2022, Broadcom announced its acquisition of VMware in a deal valued at approximately $61 billion — one of the largest technology acquisitions in history. When the deal closed in November 2023, Broadcom moved quickly to consolidate VMware's product portfolio from roughly 168 products, bundles, and editions down to a small number of primary subscription bundles. The Unified Cloud Pack program I worked on was part of the commercial infrastructure that made that consolidation operationally possible: before products can be bundled, retired, or restructured at scale, they have to be correctly defined in the systems that govern how they are quoted, invoiced, and provisioned. Getting the Offers domain right was not just a feature release — it was foundational plumbing for one of the most consequential product restructurings in enterprise software.

03 · Understanding the Ecosystem

Two lifecycles, not one

The biggest lesson from this assignment: enterprise product management runs on two interconnected lifecycles, and most of the interesting work happens at the seam between them.

Lifecycle 1 — Product Development

A business opportunity becomes a working capability through Agile delivery. When VMware identified the opportunity to simplify infrastructure purchasing, Product Managers, Product Owners, Engineering, Architects, and Business stakeholders collaborated to define requirements, prioritize the backlog, and deliver incrementally.

The flow
Business Need→ Product Discovery→ Backlog Prioritization→ Sprint Planning→ Development→ Release

As an Associate Product Owner in the Offers Team, I contributed here — refining requirements, managing backlog items, supporting sprint planning, and validating acceptance criteria alongside Engineering.

Lifecycle 2 — Enterprise Commerce

Once a capability shipped, a second lifecycle began: the commercial offer's journey through VMware's commerce ecosystem before it ever reached a customer. Say VMware wanted to launch a new Cloud Pack bundling vSphere Enterprise, vSAN Enterprise, and NSX. The business team defined the commercial strategy — what belonged in the bundle, who could buy it, pricing rules, migration paths, entitlement policy. The Offers Team then translated that into a structured commercial offer inside Oracle PDH, which became the enterprise source of truth.

The flow
Commercial Offer→ Oracle PDH→ CPQ→ SAP→ Commerce Portal→ Licensing & Provisioning→ Customer

Why this mattered: even a small change within the Offers domain could ripple through every downstream platform involved in a customer's purchasing journey. Successful enterprise product management isn't just building features — it's understanding how a commercial decision moves through an organization's operational ecosystem.

04 · My Role

Owning the Offers domain

As an Associate Product Owner within VMware's Offers Team, I owned the product domain that managed commercial offers and product configurations. Rather than building products like vSphere or NSX, our team built the commercial capabilities that let customers purchase those products as subscription offerings such as Unified Cloud Pack.

My responsibilities included:

  • Managing and prioritizing the Offers product backlog — deciding which offer configurations, entitlement rules, and bundle structures were built first, based on launch timelines and cross-team dependencies.
  • Translating commercial strategy into structured requirements — working with business stakeholders to convert pricing decisions and bundle designs into user stories that Engineering could implement inside Oracle PDH.
  • Defining and validating acceptance criteria — confirming that each offer definition was complete, accurate, and consumable by downstream systems before it was released.
  • Coordinating across Engineering, SAP, CPQ, and Business Operations — managing the dependencies between teams whose timelines and priorities didn't always align.
  • Supporting sprint planning, backlog refinement, and release readiness reviews throughout the Agile delivery cycle.

The role required balancing business priorities with technical feasibility — while keeping product information accurate and consistent across the entire commerce ecosystem.

05 · End-to-End Product Flow

From business strategy to customer purchase

Unified Cloud Pack is a good example of how an enterprise commercial offering moves from idea to customer purchase, in four stages.

1
Business Opportunity
VMware identified the opportunity to simplify infrastructure purchasing by packaging products like vSphere, vSAN, and NSX into a single subscription offering instead of selling them individually.
2
Product Development
Product Managers, Product Owners, Engineering, and Business stakeholders defined the commercial requirements, pricing strategy, migration rules, and customer experience. I contributed by refining requirements, managing backlog items, and validating acceptance criteria.
3
Commercial Offer Design
The Offers Team modeled the commercial bundle within Oracle PDH — the enterprise source of truth for product relationships, subscription rules, entitlement structures, and commercial attributes.
4
Enterprise Commerce Flow
Once published, the offer flowed through VMware's commerce platform: Oracle PDH stored and distributed it, CPQ configured products and generated quotations, SAP managed contracts and billing, Commerce presented the offering to customers, and Licensing & Provisioning activated entitlements after purchase.

What this meant for customers: a simple subscription purchase, with five enterprise systems quietly agreeing behind the screen. My role was making sure the commercial definitions created in the Offers domain accurately represented business intent — and could be reliably consumed by every platform downstream.

06 · Key Challenges

Coordination, not implementation, was the hard part

A seemingly small change to a commercial offer supporting Unified Cloud Pack could affect multiple downstream systems at once — and each team came to the table with a different priority.

Engineering
Focused on implementation
SAP
Pricing integrity & financial accuracy
Offers Team
Single source of truth
CPQ
Quotation logic
Business
Customer adoption & commercial outcomes

The Offers Team sat at the center of these interactions. The tension became most visible during a mid-program sprint when a business stakeholder requested a last-minute change to the entitlement structure for one of the Cloud Pack tiers — a commercial decision that, on the surface, seemed contained. In practice, it required corresponding updates in CPQ's product configuration rules, SAP's contract pricing logic, and the Commerce portal's display metadata. Engineering had already built and validated the original definition. CPQ had already scheduled a release. The SAP team had billing logic in test. A unilateral update to PDH at that stage would have created inconsistencies across four systems simultaneously. Rather than block the change or accept it uncritically, I worked with the business stakeholder to understand the commercial reason behind the request, assessed the downstream impact with each team lead, and proposed a phased implementation: the PDH definition update would go into the next sprint cycle, with coordinated downstream changes sequenced across CPQ and SAP in the following two sprints. The commercial outcome the business needed was preserved — the implementation risk was controlled.

07 · Cross-Functional Collaboration

The Offers Team as the bridge

Unified Cloud Pack's success depended on close collaboration across Product Management, Engineering, SAP, CPQ, Business Operations, and the Offers Team. Regular backlog refinement sessions, sprint planning meetings, stakeholder reviews, release demonstrations, and release-readiness discussions surfaced dependencies early and kept product changes aligned with business priorities.

Rather than working in isolation, the Offers Team acted as the bridge between commercial strategy and technical execution — making sure every downstream platform received consistent, accurate product information.

10 · Reflection

Built through collaboration, not individual ownership

My 18 months at VMware taught me that successful enterprise transformations are built through coordination, not individual brilliance — and that the coordination is harder than it looks from the outside. Supporting Unified Cloud Pack gave me direct experience of how a commercial idea travels through an organisation before it reaches a customer: from a business stakeholder's pricing decision, through product definition in Oracle PDH, through CPQ configuration, SAP billing logic, Commerce display, and finally into a customer's licensing environment. Each hand-off is a potential breakage point. The job of a product owner in an enterprise domain is not to build features — it's to make hand-offs reliable.

If I were approaching this work again, I would invest earlier in shared definition standards across systems. Much of the coordination overhead came from each downstream team having slightly different expectations about what a "complete" offer definition looked like. Establishing a shared definition-of-done for PDH records — agreed upfront with CPQ, SAP, and Commerce leads — would have reduced rework significantly in the early sprints. That lesson has shaped how I approach cross-system product work: alignment on what "done" means is more valuable than any individual sprint velocity improvement.

I also came away with a clearer picture of where AI will change this kind of work. The majority of the coordination effort I described — tracking dependencies, checking consistency across systems, flagging when a change in one domain has downstream consequences — is exactly the kind of pattern recognition that AI-assisted product tooling is beginning to automate. The PM of this kind of domain five years from now will spend less time on synchronisation and more time on the commercial decisions that require judgment. I think that's the right direction.
