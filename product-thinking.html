Product Philosophy

How I think about building

01
Slow down in discovery to go fast in delivery
Every week I spend with users before writing a line of spec saves two weeks of rework after. The Ed-Fi migration nearly shipped as an XML wrapper because the team hadn't spoken to a single district IT director about the real pain. I insisted on 11 interviews first. That changed the architecture, the rollout plan, and ultimately the outcome. Speed in PM is upstream, not downstream.
02
Quantify the cost of inaction, not just the cost of action
Every investment decision I bring to leadership includes a "what happens if we don't" column. The TCO model for the Ed-Fi migration wasn't just about the migration cost — it modelled the compounding maintenance debt of the alternative. That reframe is what got an expensive architectural decision approved fast. PMs who only cost out the thing they want usually lose to the status quo.
03
Great PM work leaves a clear decision trail
I write down every non-trivial product decision, what alternatives were considered, what data we had, and what we decided. Not for process — for institutional memory. Six months after shipping, someone will ask "why did we build it this way?" and the answer should exist in writing. Decision trails are how a PM's thinking outlives their tenure on a team.
04
Compliance products are a UX problem, not just a technical one
In regulated industries — EdTech, FinTech, GovTech — the temptation is to build for the regulator, not the user. But the school IT director filing data to the state is a user too. She has deadlines, stress, and limited technical bandwidth. Every hour my platform saves her is product value. The best compliance products feel effortless to the humans in the middle.
05
AI changes what PMs must know, not just what they can do
Using AI tools to prototype faster is a productivity habit. Actually being an AI PM means understanding precision/recall tradeoffs, hallucination failure modes, latency budgets, and how to write acceptance criteria for probabilistic systems. The bar for AI PM is rising fast — I'm deliberately building technical depth, not just tool familiarity.
06
The best product metric is one the business would miss if it disappeared
DAU/MAU is a ratio. Churn rate is a rate. These matter — but the metric I care most about is the one where, if it moves the wrong way, someone in finance or CS calls me directly. At PowerSchool, that was the number of at-risk accounts by Gainsight health tier. If it went up, revenue was at risk. That's a real product metric.
PM Toolkit

Frameworks I actually use

🎯
Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)
When: Discovery · Structuring research
Teresa Torres' framework for connecting desired outcomes → opportunities → solutions → experiments. Changed how I run discovery sprints — I now map every user interview finding to an opportunity node before jumping to solutions.
Used on: Ed-Fi migration discovery · PowerSchool VoC programme
📊
RICE Scoring
When: Roadmap prioritisation · Backlog grooming
Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort. I use this every sprint to force a conversation about Confidence — teams that skip confidence end up over-indexing on features they believe in emotionally rather than evidentially. The denominator matters as much as the numerators.
Used on: PowerSchool Q1 2025 roadmap · VMware backlog (200+ items)
🔄
Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)
When: User research synthesis · Positioning
"When I ___, I want to ___, so I can ___." The job framing forces you to think about context and motivation, not just features. The district IT director doesn't want "real-time validation" — she wants to stop finding out about failures at 8am the next day when she's already in a meeting.
Used on: Ed-Fi validation feature design
📋
Given / When / Then (Acceptance Criteria)
When: PRD writing · Sprint planning
Structured acceptance criteria that specify preconditions, actions, and expected outcomes. Forces engineers and QA to have the same mental model of "done." The most common PRD failure I see is acceptance criteria that describe intent, not observable behaviour — GWT fixes that.
Used on: VMware Cloud Pack PRD · Every PRD I've written in the last 3 years
💰
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) Modelling
When: Build vs. buy · Architecture decisions
Model the 2–3 year total cost of each option including maintenance, opportunity cost, and technical debt — not just upfront build cost. The Ed-Fi migration was approved on a TCO argument, not a technical one. Leadership approved money, not architecture.
Used on: Ed-Fi vs. XML wrapper decision · 3+ architecture decisions at PowerSchool
🤖
AI Error Surface Analysis
When: AI feature PRDs · Model evaluation
My own framework: for any AI feature, map the failure modes along two axes — detectability (is the error obvious or silent?) and severity (what's the worst-case consequence?). Features in the "silent + severe" quadrant need mandatory human review gates. Features in "obvious + low-severity" can fail gracefully in the UI.
Used on: PowerSchool LLM compliance summary feature
Mental Models

How I think — borrowed from everywhere

Inversion Charlie Munger
Instead of asking "how do we succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" Then don't do those things. Applied to product: before prioritising a feature, I ask "what would make this feature cause harm to users?" The answers are usually more useful than the original question.
Used on: Ed-Fi migration risk analysis · VMware entitlement zero-degradation requirement
Second-Order Thinking Howard Marks
What happens next? And then what happens after that? First-order thinking is "this feature will increase engagement." Second-order: "increased engagement means more data stored — does our data architecture support that at scale? What's the privacy implication?" PMs who stop at the first order miss the surprises.
Used on: LLM feature evaluation at PowerSchool
Reversibility Test Jeff Bezos / Two-way doors
Type 1 decisions (irreversible) require careful deliberation. Type 2 decisions (reversible) should be made fast and corrected if wrong. The mistake most PM teams make is treating Type 2 decisions as Type 1 — analysing for months when they could ship, measure, and iterate. The Ed-Fi migration was Type 1; almost everything else was Type 2.
Applied to: Roadmap prioritisation · PRD scope decisions · Team decision velocity
Gall's Law John Gall, Systemantics
"A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked." The corollary: a complex system designed from scratch never works. This is why I insist on MVPs even when stakeholders want full-featured launches. The Ed-Fi dual-write approach (simple parallel system first) is Gall's Law in practice.
Applied to: Ed-Fi migration phased rollout · PowerSchool AI feature scoping
Chesterton's Fence G.K. Chesterton
Don't remove something until you understand why it was put there. When I joined PowerSchool, the XML architecture looked obviously wrong — until I understood why it existed: it was the only format some legacy state reporting systems could accept. Knowing that changed the migration timeline and risk model significantly.
Applied to: Ed-Fi migration discovery · Every architecture "simplification" I've evaluated
Regret Minimisation Jeff Bezos
When making a hard career or product decision: imagine yourself at 80 looking back. Which choice would you regret more — having tried and failed, or not having tried? I used this when deciding to push for the Ed-Fi full migration over the wrapper. The wrapper would have been the "safe" choice. I wouldn't have been proud of it in five years.
Applied to: Ed-Fi architecture decision · Career move from L&T → MBA → PM
